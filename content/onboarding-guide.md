# Facilitator Onboarding Guide

First-time facilitator orientation for Founder Jam. Read this before `facilitator-playbook.md` if this is your first event.

---

## What Founder Jam Is

Founder Jam is a full-day, hands-on workshop for early-stage founders. The goal is not to teach a curriculum -- it is to get founders doing real work that moves their business forward in a single day.

Co-hosted by Innovate Charlotte and StealthX, Founder Jam runs in two tracks:
- **Product Track:** Competitive landscape, strategic hypotheses, and a working prototype
- **Growth Track:** ICP definition, automation workflows, and a 30-day validation plan

Each startup team is assigned one dedicated facilitator for the entire day. You are not a speaker or a teacher. You are a guide, a provocateur, and an accountability partner.

---

## What Your Role Is (and Is Not)

| You Are | You Are Not |
|---------|------------|
| A guide for one startup all day | A teacher delivering a lecture |
| A provocateur asking hard questions | A validator confirming their ideas |
| An accountability partner | A consultant doing the work for them |
| A systems thinker pushing beyond tactics | A technical co-founder building their product |

**The most common mistake new facilitators make:** Solving problems instead of asking questions. When you see a founder stuck, the instinct is to give the answer. Resist it. Ask a question instead. The founder who arrives at the insight themselves will act on it. The founder who receives the answer often does not.

## Framework Fluency

You do not need to be an expert in every framework, but you need to be fluent enough to recognize when a team is using one well versus using it as a crutch. Internalize these:

### Rumelt's Good Strategy / Bad Strategy Kernel
Good strategy has three parts: a diagnosis (what's the problem?), a guiding policy (how will we approach it?), and coherent actions (what specifically will we do?). Bad strategy is a list of goals dressed up as a plan.

Watch for: Founders who have goals but no diagnosis. Ask "Why does this problem exist?" before "What will you do?"

### Jobs-to-be-Done (Christensen / Moesta)
People don't buy products -- they hire them to do a job. The JTBD statement: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]."

Watch for: ICP definitions based on demographics instead of situations and motivations. Push from "HR directors at mid-size companies" to "HR directors facing open enrollment who can't absorb another benefit cut."

### Double Diamond
Design Council's diverge-then-converge framework. Phase 1: explore widely (diverge), then define the problem (converge). Phase 2: develop options (diverge), then deliver a solution (converge).

Watch for: Teams that converge too early. A team that picked the first idea they had in the first 20 minutes did not explore.

### Customer Development (Blank)
Hypotheses exist to be tested, not confirmed. The goal is to find out what is not working as fast as possible. "Get out of the building" means test assumptions with real people before building.

Watch for: Prototypes designed to impress rather than to learn. Ask "What assumption does this prototype test?"

## Role Descriptions

### Lead Facilitator
- Frames the day, teaches the concepts, keeps timing on track
- Floats between tables to unblock teams
- Runs the panel and emcees the presentations at the end of the day
- Makes judgment calls on agenda flexing based on energy and momentum

### Table Facilitator (Your Role)
- Works directly with one startup the entire day
- Asks sharp questions, pokes holes, keeps the team in double-diamond rhythm
- Translates ambiguity into next actions -- many founders are new to design thinking
- Escalates genuinely stuck situations to the lead facilitator
- May roam to help other tables if your startup is executing well independently

### Roaming Support
- Floats between tables to provide technical help (tool setup, API issues)
- Does not do deep facilitation -- defers to the assigned table facilitator
- Handles logistics (power strips, supplies, food)

## What to Prepare the Week Before

**Know your assigned startup.** Review their intake form or pre-event survey. Know their industry, stage, team size, and what they said they want to leave with.

**Know which track they're in.** Product Track and Growth Track have different schedules and activities. Read the relevant sections of `facilitator-playbook.md`.

**Prepare 3-5 pointed questions.** Based on their intake form, write 3-5 questions you want to push them on during the day. What assumptions seem fragile? What's missing from their framing?

**Know the founder repo resources.** The sibling `founderjamskills` repo contains resources you should be aware of:

| File | What It Contains |
|------|-----------------|
| `templates/battle-card-template.md` | Battle card structure founders fill in |
| `templates/hypothesis-template.md` | "If / then / by" hypothesis format |
| `templates/icp-template.md` | ICP canvas with JTBD and scoring |
| `templates/nurture-sequence-template.md` | Email sequence structure |
| `templates/pitch-outline-template.md` | End-of-day share-out structure |
| `templates/30-day-plan-template.md` | Week-by-week validation plan |
| `content/hypothesis-thought-starters.md` | 20 hypothesis thought starters for Product Track Activity 2 |
| `content/prototype-thought-starters.md` | 14 prototype types for Product Track Activity 3 |
| `content/frameworks-reference.md` | Extended framework explanations (Rumelt, JTBD, Double Diamond, Blank) |
| `content/facilitator-questions.md` | Canonical question bank for all skills |

When a founder asks "what should this look like?" -- point them to the relevant template in their repo.

**Know the tools.** Review `content/tools-reference.md`. You do not need to be an expert in every tool, but you should know which tools are used for which tasks and roughly how they work.

**Know the prompts.** Skim `content/ai-prompts-library.md`. When a founder is stuck on ICP or can't write an email sequence, knowing which prompt to reach for is faster than improvising.

**Get good sleep.** Facilitation is cognitively demanding. You are running 6-9 hours of close, focused engagement. Show up rested.

## Ground Rules for the Day

These apply to you as a facilitator, not just to founders:

- **Be present.** Don't check your phone while your team is working.
- **Take notes.** Write down what the founders say, not just what you say. You will use these for follow-up.
- **Name the dynamic.** If your team is going in circles, say it: "I notice we've revisited this three times. Let's commit to one direction and move."
- **Celebrate small wins.** Founders discount their progress. When something clicks, name it.
- **Escalate early.** If your team is genuinely blocked -- technical issue you can't solve, interpersonal conflict, complete confusion -- get the lead facilitator involved. Don't let the team sit stuck for more than 10-15 minutes.

## Common Mistakes New Facilitators Make

**1. Doing the work instead of coaching.**
You write the ICP, you draft the email, you map the workflow. The founder nods along. They leave with an output they don't understand and can't operate. Solution: Ask "What would you write here?" before you write anything.

**2. Being too gentle.**
You don't push back on a vague answer because you don't want to make the founder uncomfortable. The session stays at a surface level. The founder leaves with polished language around a weak idea. Solution: "That's interesting, but I'm not sure I believe it. Walk me through why."

**3. Losing track of time.**
You get deep into a fascinating conversation about competitive strategy and miss the transition to session 2. Your team is now 30 minutes behind and rushed for the rest of the day. Solution: Set a timer. Visible urgency is your friend.

**4. Validating what they want to hear.**
A founder presents their idea with obvious emotional investment. You find the strengths instead of the gaps. They leave more confident in a fragile idea. Solution: Ask "What would prove you wrong?" before "What makes this good?"

**5. Over-facilitating.**
You ask so many questions that the founder can't get into a flow state to do actual work. The session becomes an interview, not a working session. Solution: Ask a sharp question, then step back. Give them 10-15 minutes to work before re-engaging.

**6. Not escalating when stuck.**
You spend 30 minutes trying to solve a technical issue that the lead facilitator could resolve in 5. Solution: Give it 10 minutes. If you're not making progress, surface it immediately.
